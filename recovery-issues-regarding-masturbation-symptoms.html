Recovery doubt

Recovery is a topic of great interest among brothers. Many start rebooting because of physical issues, hoping that by doing so, they can regain their health. Whether others recover or not can impact one’s confidence in reboot. If it seems that many do not recover, one might lose the motivation and confidence to continue. The plateau period in recovery

Some brothers do engage in wellness activities, but they might feel their recovery has stalled after reaching a certain point, which is actually encountering a plateau period. It’s normal to hit such a plateau. To break through, one should seek methods, much like overcoming a technical difficulty; advancing in wellness methods and awareness is necessary.

This situation is similar to running: if you work hard and reach 12 seconds in the 100-meter dash, you might find yourself stuck at 12 seconds without further improvement. Having an experienced coach would be beneficial then. A coach can guide you on how to break through the plateau and further improve your performance to reach 11 seconds or even 10 seconds. I read an article by a track coach who stated that professional athletes need at least three years of systematic training to run under 10 seconds, exceptional talents notwithstanding.

If we encounter a plateau in recovery, we should study more wellness articles, try various wellness practices, and find the one that suits us best to stick with. For instance, standing meditation — if you can master it and persist, many physical symptoms can be greatly improved. There are also real cases of people alleviating or eliminating diseases through Tai Chi or yoga practice. Dietary supplements are good, but not as effective as exercise supplements; knowing how to exercise properly is a major topic. Many know aerobic exercise is beneficial and run daily, sweating profusely, only to find themselves weakened and fatigued, lacking energy. Traditional Chinese medicine is correct in saying excessive sweating harms yang energy. Young individuals with strong constitutions might sustain this, but it can lead to problems if continued. The Ma’s Army athletics team placed a high priority on post-exercise recovery and conditioning, daily brewing medicinal soups for the athletes.

My recovery also involved continually breaking through plateaus. I read about heel raises stimulating the Chengshan acupoint on the calves, similar to the “Shake Seven Times at the Back” movement in the Eight Section Brocade exercises, so I did it hundreds, even thousands of times daily, even while walking. However, when practicing any technique, one must grasp the essentials and avoid blind, aimless practice. For example, some brothers heard squats were beneficial and practiced 500 a day, only to end up damaging their knees. When squatting, the knees should not exceed the toes, and both should point in the same direction to avoid damaging the knees.
